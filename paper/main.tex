% ABSTRACT
% ======================================================================
\begin{abstract}
Game-theoretic formulations of reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF)---including NLHF~\citep{munos2024nash}, INPO~\citep{inpo2025}, and \citet{starlhf2024}---model alignment as a strategic interaction between a policy and an adversary, yet all operate exclusively in discrete time.
We reformulate alignment as a \emph{continuous-time two-player zero-sum differential game} and show that reward hacking has the structure of a \emph{Hopf bifurcation}: there exists a critical KL penalty~$\beta_c$ below which gradient play produces sustained oscillations at a computable frequency~$\omega_c$, while the Nash equilibrium remains stable for all~$\beta$.
The bifurcation thus characterizes the gap between gradient-based training (which reward-hacks) and equilibrium play (which does not).
We prove supercriticality via center manifold reduction, characterize the Nash equilibrium through an algebraic Riccati equation, and show that the bifurcation persists in a nonlinear softmax alignment game with distribution-shift reward corruption, demonstrating that the phenomenon extends beyond the linear-quadratic framework.
\end{abstract}

% 1. INTRODUCTION

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) has become the dominant paradigm for aligning large language models with human intent~\citep{ouyang2022training,bai2022training}.
The standard pipeline---train a reward model on preference data, then optimize the policy against this proxy reward while penalizing divergence from a reference policy via a KL term---can be understood as an \emph{implicit game}: the policy seeks to maximize reward while the reward model (or, more broadly, the environment) may present an adversarial distribution over queries.
This game-theoretic view has been made explicit in a series of recent works.
\citet{munos2024nash} introduced Nash Learning from Human Feedback (NLHF), casting alignment as a two-player constant-sum game between the policy and a ``dueling'' comparator, with the solution concept being a Nash equilibrium in the space of mixed policies.
\citet{inpo2025} proposed \emph{INPO}, an iterative Nash policy optimization method based on no-regret learning that avoids explicit win-rate estimation.
\citet{starlhf2024} framed preference learning as a zero-sum game and showed that a single agent playing against itself can find the minimax winner without a reward model.
A complementary line of work studies reward over-optimization empirically~\citep{gao2023scaling}, finding scaling laws that characterize how excess proxy reward grows with KL budget.

Despite their conceptual appeal, all existing game-theoretic RLHF formulations operate in \emph{discrete time}: updates occur at iteration indices $k = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$, and convergence is analyzed via contraction arguments or potential functions on the iterate sequence.
This discrete viewpoint obscures three phenomena that become transparent in the continuous-time limit:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em,itemsep=1pt,topsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{Equilibrium geometry.} The Nash equilibrium of the alignment game, when it exists, is the solution of a Hamilton--Jacobi--Isaacs PDE whose characteristics reveal the curvature of the reward landscape and the geometry of the policy manifold.
  \item \textbf{Exact cycling thresholds.} The transition from convergent training to periodic reward-hacking oscillations is a \emph{Hopf bifurcation}---a codimension-one phenomenon with a sharp critical parameter $\beta_c$. While discrete-time systems have an analogous Neimark--Sacker bifurcation, the continuous-time formulation yields cleaner expressions for the threshold and frequency, and connects directly to classical optimal control tools.
  \item \textbf{Optimal schedules via optimal control.} Pontryagin's Maximum Principle (PMP) transforms the problem of finding the best KL penalty schedule into a boundary-value ODE, motivating an analytically structured schedule in the LQ regime.
\end{enumerate}

Our approach connects to the ``deep learning as optimal control'' paradigm initiated by \citet{e2017proposal}, who observed that residual networks are discretizations of controlled ODEs.
This perspective was developed further by \citet{li2018optimal}, who applied Pontryagin's maximum principle to training, and the Neural ODE framework~\citep{chen2018neural}, which parameterizes continuous dynamics directly.
We adapt these ideas from the supervised to the RLHF setting, replacing a single controlled ODE with a \emph{two-player differential game}.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
We make three contributions, each with a precise theorem:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em,itemsep=1pt,topsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{Hopf bifurcation as the mechanism of reward hacking} (Theorem~\ref{thm:hopf}): We show that gradient play in the alignment game undergoes a supercritical Hopf bifurcation at a critical $\beta_c$.
    For $\beta > \beta_c$, gradient play converges; for $\beta < \beta_c$, it produces sustained reward-hacking oscillations at a computable frequency~$\omega_c$.
    The Nash equilibrium itself is always stable---the bifurcation characterizes the gap between gradient-based training and equilibrium play.
    We prove supercriticality by computing the first Lyapunov coefficient via center manifold reduction ($\ell_1 = -0.0518 < 0$ for our 4D model).
  \item \textbf{Nash equilibrium via Riccati} (Theorem~\ref{thm:riccati}): The continuous-time alignment game admits an LQ approximation whose Nash equilibrium is characterized by an algebraic Riccati equation~\citep{basar1999dynamic}.
    The contribution is the \emph{formulation}---mapping RLHF dynamics onto this framework---not the Riccati theory itself.
  \item \textbf{Schedule analysis} (Proposition~\ref{prop:schedule}): The bifurcation analysis explains why non-constant KL schedules outperform constant baselines: schedules that start above $\beta_c$ and decay avoid the unstable regime.
    A PMP-motivated ansatz captures the structural form (damped oscillation at the natural frequency), though simple heuristics like cosine annealing achieve lower regret in practice.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Preliminaries}
\label{sec:prelim}

\paragraph{Two-player zero-sum differential games.}
Consider two players with controls $\bu(t) \in \R^m$ and $\bv(t) \in \R^n$ influencing a state $\bx(t) \in \R^d$ through dynamics $\dot{\bx} = f(\bx, \bu, \bv, t)$, $\bx(0) = \bx_0$.
Player~1 (the \emph{maximizer}) seeks to maximize, and Player~2 (the \emph{minimizer}) seeks to minimize, the payoff
\begin{equation}
  \cJ[\bu, \bv] = \int_0^T \ell(\bx, \bu, \bv, t)\,dt + \Phi(\bx(T)).
  \label{eq:payoff}
\end{equation}
A pair $(\bu^*, \bv^*)$ is a \emph{saddle-point (Nash) equilibrium} if $\cJ[\bu, \bv^*] \leq \cJ[\bu^*, \bv^*] \leq \cJ[\bu^*, \bv]$ for all admissible $\bu, \bv$.

\paragraph{Pontryagin's Maximum Principle.}
Define the Hamiltonian $\cH(\bx, \bu, \bv, \bp, t) = \ell(\bx, \bu, \bv, t) + \bp^\top f(\bx, \bu, \bv, t)$ with costate $\bp(t) \in \R^d$.
At a saddle point, the PMP conditions are: $\dot{\bx} = \nabla_{\bp}\cH$, $\dot{\bp} = -\nabla_{\bx}\cH$, $\nabla_{\bu}\cH = 0$ (maximization), and $\nabla_{\bv}\cH = 0$ (minimization), with boundary condition $\bp(T) = \nabla\Phi(\bx(T))$.

\paragraph{Hamilton--Jacobi--Isaacs equation.}
The \emph{value function} $V(\bx, t) = \sup_{\bu}\inf_{\bv}\cJ[\bu, \bv \mid \bx, t]$ satisfies the HJI PDE
\begin{equation}
  -\pder{V}{t} = \sup_{\bu}\inf_{\bv}\bigl[\ell(\bx, \bu, \bv, t) + (\nabla_{\bx}V)^\top f(\bx, \bu, \bv, t)\bigr],
  \label{eq:hji}
\end{equation}
with terminal condition $V(\bx, T) = \Phi(\bx)$.
If the Isaacs condition holds---the order of $\sup$ and $\inf$ can be interchanged---then the game has a value.

\paragraph{RLHF notation.}
Let $\pi_\btheta$ denote a policy parameterized by $\btheta \in \R^{d_\theta}$, $\pi_{\mathrm{ref}}$ a reference policy, $r_\bphi$ a reward model parameterized by $\bphi \in \R^{d_\phi}$, and $r^*$ the true (latent) reward.
Standard RLHF maximizes $\E_{\pi_\btheta}[r_\bphi(x,y)] - \beta\,\KL(\pi_\btheta \| \pi_{\mathrm{ref}})$.
In the game-theoretic view, $\btheta$ is controlled by the policy player and $\bphi$ by the adversary, which models online reward model corruption from distributional shift: as the policy moves, the training distribution changes, degrading the reward model's accuracy in directions the policy exploits.

\section{Continuous-Time Alignment Game}
\label{sec:game}

We model alignment as a continuous-time, two-player, zero-sum differential game over a finite horizon $[0, T]$.

\paragraph{State and dynamics.}
Define the joint state $\bz(t) = (\btheta(t), \bphi(t)) \in \R^d$, $d = d_\theta + d_\phi$.
The policy player chooses a control $\bu(t) \in \R^{d_\theta}$ (gradient-based update direction for $\btheta$) and the adversary chooses $\bv(t) \in \R^{d_\phi}$ (perturbation to the reward model or query distribution).
The dynamics are
\begin{equation}
  \dot{\bz}(t) = \bF(\bz, \bu, \bv, t) =
  \begin{pmatrix}
    \nabla_\btheta \cL(\btheta, \bphi) + \bB_1 \bu \\[3pt]
    g(\btheta, \bphi) + \bB_2 \bv
  \end{pmatrix},
  \quad \bz(0) = \bz_0,
  \label{eq:dynamics}
\end{equation}
where $\cL(\btheta, \bphi) = \E_{\pi_\btheta}[r_\bphi] - \beta\,\KL(\pi_\btheta \| \pi_{\mathrm{ref}})$ is the RLHF objective, $g(\btheta, \bphi)$ captures the reward model's natural drift (e.g., online reward learning or distributional shift), and $\bB_1 \in \R^{d_\theta \times d_\theta}$, $\bB_2 \in \R^{d_\phi \times d_\phi}$ are input matrices scaling the control authority of each player.

\paragraph{Payoff functional.}
The policy player maximizes
\begin{equation}
  \cJ[\bu, \bv] = \int_0^T \bigl[\underbrace{r^*(\btheta)}_{\text{true reward}} - \underbrace{\beta(t)\,\KL(\pi_\btheta \| \pi_{\mathrm{ref}})}_{\text{KL penalty}} - \underbrace{\tfrac{\gamma}{2}\|\bu\|^2}_{\text{policy effort}} + \underbrace{\tfrac{\delta}{2}\|\bv\|^2}_{\text{adversary cost}}\bigr]\,dt + \Phi(\bz(T)),
  \label{eq:payoff_alignment}
\end{equation}
where $\gamma > 0$ penalizes aggressive policy updates, $\delta > 0$ penalizes large adversarial perturbations (reflecting limited adversary budget), and $\Phi$ is a terminal payoff.
The adversary minimizes the same functional: this is zero-sum.

\paragraph{Hamiltonian and PMP optimality.}
Define costates $\bp_\btheta(t) \in \R^{d_\theta}$, $\bp_\bphi(t) \in \R^{d_\phi}$, and the Hamiltonian
\begin{equation}
  \cH = r^*(\btheta) - \beta\,\KL(\pi_\btheta\|\pi_{\mathrm{ref}}) - \tfrac{\gamma}{2}\|\bu\|^2 + \tfrac{\delta}{2}\|\bv\|^2 + \bp_\btheta^\top\bigl(\nabla_\btheta \cL + \bB_1 \bu\bigr) + \bp_\bphi^\top\bigl(g + \bB_2 \bv\bigr).
  \label{eq:hamiltonian}
\end{equation}
Stationarity $\nabla_\bu \cH = 0$ and $\nabla_\bv \cH = 0$ yield the optimal controls
\begin{equation}
  \bu^*(t) = \frac{1}{\gamma}\,\bB_1^\top \bp_\btheta(t), \qquad
  \bv^*(t) = -\frac{1}{\delta}\,\bB_2^\top \bp_\bphi(t).
  \label{eq:optimal_controls}
\end{equation}
The signs confirm the saddle-point structure: the policy player moves \emph{along} the costate (ascending), while the adversary moves \emph{against} it (descending).

\paragraph{HJI PDE.}
Substituting the optimal controls into~\eqref{eq:hji}, the value function $V(\bz, t)$ satisfies
\begin{equation}
  -\pder{V}{t} = r^* - \beta\,\KL + (\nabla_\bz V)^\top \bF_0(\bz) + \frac{1}{2\gamma}\|\bB_1^\top \nabla_\btheta V\|^2 - \frac{1}{2\delta}\|\bB_2^\top \nabla_\bphi V\|^2,
  \label{eq:hji_alignment}
\end{equation}
where $\bF_0(\bz) = (\nabla_\btheta \cL,\; g(\btheta, \bphi))^\top$ is the uncontrolled drift.
The opposing signs of the last two terms reflect the game's zero-sum nature: the policy player benefits from a large gradient of the value w.r.t.\ $\btheta$, while the adversary benefits from a large gradient w.r.t.\ $\bphi$.

\paragraph{LQ approximation.}
Let $\bz^* = (\btheta^*, \bphi^*)$ be a stationary point satisfying $\bF_0(\bz^*) = 0$.
Setting $\tilde{\bz} = \bz - \bz^*$ and linearizing:
\begin{equation}
  \dot{\tilde{\bz}} = \bA\,\tilde{\bz} + \bB_{1,\mathrm{f}} \bu + \bB_{2,\mathrm{f}} \bv, \qquad
  \bA = \nabla_\bz \bF_0\big|_{\bz^*} =
  \begin{pmatrix}
    \bA_{11} & \bA_{12} \\
    \bA_{21} & \bA_{22}
  \end{pmatrix},
  \label{eq:lq_dynamics}
\end{equation}
where $\bB_{1,\mathrm{f}} = [\bB_1; \bm{0}]$ and $\bB_{2,\mathrm{f}} = [\bm{0}; \bB_2]$ are the zero-padded control matrices over the full state $\tilde{\bz} = (\tilde{\btheta}, \tilde{\bphi})$.
The quadratic payoff (Player 1 maximizes, Player 2 minimizes) is $J = \int_0^T \bigl\{-\tfrac{1}{2}\tilde{\bz}^\top \widetilde{\bQ}\,\tilde{\bz} - \tfrac{\gamma}{2}\|\bu\|^2 + \tfrac{\delta}{2}\|\bv\|^2\bigr\}\,dt$, where $\widetilde{\bQ} = -\nabla^2_\bz(r^* - \beta\,\KL)\big|_{\bz^*} \succ 0$ (the negated Hessian of the concave objective) decomposes as $\widetilde{\bQ} = [[\bQ, \bm{S}_c],[\bm{S}_c^\top, \bR]]$ with $\bQ$ acting on $\tilde{\btheta}$ and $\bR$ on $\tilde{\bphi}$.
This is a standard LQ differential game~\citep{basar1999dynamic}.

\section{Main Results}
\label{sec:results}

\begin{theorem}[Nash Equilibrium via Riccati]
\label{thm:riccati}
Consider the LQ alignment game~\eqref{eq:lq_dynamics} with cost matrix $\widetilde{\bQ} \succ 0$, policy effort $\gamma > 0$, and adversary cost $\delta > 0$.
Define $\bS = \bB_{1,\mathrm{f}}\bB_{1,\mathrm{f}}^\top/\gamma - \bB_{2,\mathrm{f}}\bB_{2,\mathrm{f}}^\top/\delta$.
The feedback (closed-loop) Nash equilibrium is characterized by the algebraic Riccati equation
\begin{equation}
  \bA^\top \bP + \bP\bA + \widetilde{\bQ} - \bP\,\bS\,\bP = 0,
  \label{eq:riccati}
\end{equation}
and the equilibrium exists if and only if~\eqref{eq:riccati} admits a stabilizing solution $\bP$ (i.e., $\bA - \bS\bP$ is Hurwitz).
The Nash equilibrium controls are $\bu^*(t) = -\frac{1}{\gamma}\bB_{1,\mathrm{f}}^\top \bP\,\tilde{\bz}(t)$ and $\bv^*(t) = \frac{1}{\delta}\bB_{2,\mathrm{f}}^\top \bP\,\tilde{\bz}(t)$, giving closed-loop dynamics $\dot{\tilde{\bz}} = (\bA - \bS\bP)\tilde{\bz}$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
In the infinite-horizon LQ game ($T \to \infty$), the maximizer's value function takes the form $V(\tilde{\bz}) = -\tfrac{1}{2}\tilde{\bz}^\top \bP\,\tilde{\bz}$ with $\bP \succ 0$ (the value is non-positive since the state cost is a penalty).
The costate is $\bp = \nabla V = -\bP\tilde{\bz}$, giving $\bu^* = \tfrac{1}{\gamma}\bB_{1,\mathrm{f}}^\top \bp = -\tfrac{1}{\gamma}\bB_{1,\mathrm{f}}^\top \bP\tilde{\bz}$.
Substituting into the HJI equation and matching quadratic coefficients yields~\eqref{eq:riccati}.
Stability of the closed-loop matrix $\bA_{\mathrm{cl}} = \bA - \bS\bP$ is necessary and sufficient for finite cost, hence for existence of the equilibrium~\citep{basar1999dynamic}.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Hopf Bifurcation in Gradient Play]
\label{thm:hopf}
Consider the \emph{open-loop} (gradient play) dynamics $\dot{\tilde{\bz}} = \bA(\beta)\tilde{\bz}$, representing the regime where policy and reward model follow gradient updates without equilibrium feedback.
Let $\bA(\beta)$ depend on $\beta$ through $\bA_{11,\mathrm{eff}} = \bA_{11} - \beta\,\bI$, and assume $(\bA_{12}\bA_{21} - \bA_{21}\bA_{12})$ is nonzero, so that the cross-coupling has a skew-symmetric component inducing rotation (verified for the models in \S\ref{sec:experiments}).
Then there exists a critical penalty $\beta_c > 0$, determined as a zero of $\max_i \operatorname{Re}\lambda_i(\bA(\beta))$, such that:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em,itemsep=1pt]
  \item For $\beta > \beta_c$: all eigenvalues of $\bA(\beta)$ have strictly negative real part.
    The equilibrium is a stable spiral, and gradient play converges.
  \item For $\beta = \beta_c$: a pair of complex conjugate eigenvalues $\lambda_\pm = \pm i\omega_c$ crosses the imaginary axis with non-zero speed $\frac{d}{d\beta}\operatorname{Re}(\lambda_\pm)\big|_{\beta_c} \neq 0$.
    The crossing frequency is $\omega_c = |\operatorname{Im}(\lambda_\pm)|$ at $\beta = \beta_c$.
  \item For $\beta < \beta_c$: gradient play diverges from equilibrium. In the presence of nonlinear saturation (e.g., cubic terms from curvature of $r^*$), a supercritical Hopf bifurcation produces a stable limit cycle with amplitude $O(\sqrt{\beta_c - \beta})$, corresponding to periodic \emph{reward-hacking oscillations}.
\end{enumerate}
By contrast, the Nash equilibrium closed-loop $\bA_{\mathrm{cl}} = \bA - \bS\bP$ (Theorem~\ref{thm:riccati}) is Hurwitz for all $\beta > 0$ where the ARE admits a solution.
The bifurcation thus characterizes the gap between gradient play and equilibrium play: reward hacking arises precisely when practical gradient methods fail to track the Nash equilibrium.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
The $\beta$-dependence enters $\bA$ through the Hessian of the KL term, which contributes $-\beta\,\bI$ to the $\bA_{11}$ block.
As $\beta$ decreases from $+\infty$, the damping in the $\btheta$-subsystem decreases, and the cross-coupling terms $\bA_{12}, \bA_{21}$ (policy-reward interaction) create a rotational component.
We verify the transversality condition $\frac{d}{d\beta}\operatorname{Re}(\lambda)\big|_{\beta_c} \neq 0$ by implicit differentiation of the characteristic polynomial.
That $\bA - \bS\bP$ remains Hurwitz is guaranteed by the Hamiltonian eigenvalue construction of $\bP$: the stable invariant subspace of $\bm{H}$ spans the closed-loop stable manifold by design.
For the 2D model (which is the Hopf normal form with polar dynamics $\dot{r} = (a-\beta)r - \mu r^3$), the first Lyapunov coefficient is exactly $\ell_1 = -\mu = -0.2 < 0$, so the bifurcation is supercritical.
For the 4D model, we perform a center manifold reduction at $\beta_c$: since the cubic nonlinearities have no quadratic component, the Kuznetsov formula~\citep{kuznetsov2004elements} simplifies to $\ell_1 = \tfrac{1}{2\omega_c}\operatorname{Re}(\bar{\bp}^\top \bm{C}(\bm{q},\bm{q},\bar{\bm{q}}))$, where $\bm{q}$ and $\bar{\bp}$ are the right and left critical eigenvectors.
Numerical evaluation yields $\ell_1 = -0.0518 < 0$, confirming supercriticality.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[PMP-Motivated KL Schedule]
\label{prop:schedule}
Consider the outer optimization problem of choosing a time-varying penalty $\beta: [0, T] \to \R_{>0}$ to maximize the cumulative true reward $\int_0^T r^*(\btheta(t))\,dt$, where $\btheta(t)$ evolves under the closed-loop Nash dynamics of the LQ game.
Let $\mu_s > 0$ denote a decay rate (in higher-dimensional models, the spectral gap of $\bA(\beta_c)$ restricted to the stable manifold; treated as a tunable parameter in general), and let $\omega_c$ be the Hopf frequency from Theorem~\ref{thm:hopf}.
In the two-dimensional center-manifold reduction, the PMP stationarity condition motivates the ansatz
\begin{equation}
  \beta^*(t) = \beta_c\Bigl(\alpha + \eta\,e^{-\mu_s t}\cos(\omega_c t + \psi)\Bigr),
  \label{eq:beta_star}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha > 1$ is a constant offset ensuring $\beta^*(t) > \beta_c$ at all times, $\eta > 0$ controls the oscillation amplitude, $\mu_s > 0$ is a decay rate, and $\psi \in [0, 2\pi)$ is a phase.
In practice $\alpha, \eta, \mu_s, \psi$ are tuned to the system; the PMP analysis motivates the \emph{functional form} (exponentially damped oscillation around a super-critical baseline) rather than providing closed-form values for these constants.
This schedule has three properties:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em,itemsep=1pt]
  \item \textbf{Initially conservative:} $\beta^*(0) = \beta_c(\alpha + \eta\cos\psi) > \beta_c$, ensuring early-stage stability.
  \item \textbf{Asymptotic super-criticality:} $\beta^*(t) \to \alpha\beta_c > \beta_c$ as $t \to \infty$, maintaining a margin above the bifurcation threshold.
  \item \textbf{Oscillatory correction:} The $\cos(\omega_c t + \psi)$ term modulates the penalty at the system's natural frequency, damping resonant oscillation modes.
\end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}[Derivation sketch]
We treat $\beta(t)$ as an additional control in the outer problem.
The Hamiltonian of the outer problem includes a term coupling $\beta$ to the state and costate.
The stationarity condition $\partial \cH / \partial \beta = 0$ yields a two-point boundary-value problem.
Near $\beta \approx \beta_c$, the linearized adjoint system is a damped oscillator with frequency $\omega_c$ and decay rate $\mu_s$, \emph{motivating} the functional form~\eqref{eq:beta_star}.
The constants $\alpha, \eta, \mu_s, \psi$ must be tuned numerically for a given system; the PMP analysis provides the structural form rather than explicit values.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Interpretation]
The primary value of Proposition~\ref{prop:schedule} is \emph{diagnostic} rather than prescriptive: it identifies the structural features---exponential damping toward $\beta_c$, oscillation at the natural frequency $\omega_c$---that explain why simple annealing schedules work well near bifurcation points.
Constant schedules either over-regularize ($\beta \gg \beta_c$, slow convergence) or under-regularize ($\beta < \beta_c$, reward hacking).
Linear and cosine decay schedules can cross $\beta_c$, and may outperform the PMP form in practice (Table~\ref{tab:regret}).
The PMP analysis explains \emph{why} non-constant schedules help: the exponentially-damped oscillatory structure matches the system's natural dynamics near the bifurcation.
\end{remark}

% 5. REWARD HACKING AS DYNAMICAL INSTABILITY

\section{Reward Hacking as Dynamical Instability}
\label{sec:reward_hacking}

The Hopf bifurcation in Theorem~\ref{thm:hopf} provides a precise dynamical-systems interpretation of reward hacking.
Under gradient play (i.e., without Nash equilibrium feedback), the open-loop Jacobian $\bA(\beta)$ has a stable manifold $\cW^s(\bz^*)$ and, for $\beta < \beta_c$, an unstable manifold $\cW^u(\bz^*)$.
Reward hacking corresponds to a gradient-play trajectory leaving a neighborhood of $\bz^*$ along $\cW^u$: the policy increases the proxy reward $r_\bphi$ while the true reward $r^*$ decreases, producing the characteristic ``over-optimization'' curve of~\citet{gao2023scaling}.
This is the regime relevant to practical RLHF, where players follow gradient updates rather than computing and playing the Nash equilibrium at each step.

The stable manifold $\cW^s$ consists of state perturbations $\tilde{\bz}$ aligned with eigenvectors of $\bA(\beta)$ having negative real-part eigenvalues.
The unstable manifold $\cW^u$, which emerges at the Hopf bifurcation, corresponds to the directions along which the reward model's misspecification amplifies under gradient dynamics.

\begin{proposition}[Time-to-Hack Lower Bound]
\label{prop:time_to_hack}
Let $\beta < \beta_c$ and let $\lambda_{\max} = \max_i \operatorname{Re}(\lambda_i(\bA(\beta)))$ denote the maximal real part among unstable eigenvalues of the gradient-play Jacobian.
For any initial perturbation $\|\tilde{\bz}(0)\| = \epsilon$, the time for the trajectory to reach a hacking threshold $\|\tilde{\bz}(t_h)\| = \Delta$ satisfies
\begin{equation}
  t_h \geq \frac{1}{\lambda_{\max}}\ln\frac{\Delta}{\epsilon\,\kappa(\bA)},
  \label{eq:time_to_hack}
\end{equation}
where $\kappa(\bA) = \|\bm{V}\|\,\|\bm{V}^{-1}\|$ is the condition number of the eigenvector matrix $\bm{V}$ of $\bA$ (i.e., $\bA = \bm{V} \bm{\Lambda} \bm{V}^{-1}$), not the standard matrix condition number.
\end{proposition}

This bound is tight when $\tilde{\bz}(0)$ is aligned with the most unstable eigenvector.
It implies that reward hacking is slow when $\beta$ is only slightly below $\beta_c$ (since $\lambda_{\max} = O(\beta_c - \beta)$ near the bifurcation, by the transversality condition), rationalizing the empirical observation that moderate KL penalties delay but do not prevent over-optimization.

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

We validate our theoretical predictions on two families of synthetic alignment games: (1) quadratic reward games with $d_\theta = d_\phi = 2$ in both LQ and Hopf normal-form settings, and (2) a nonlinear softmax alignment game with $K=16$ actions and feature-based rewards.
Implementation details and additional experiments are in the appendix.

\paragraph{Setup.}
We define a quadratic true reward $r^*(\btheta) = -\tfrac{1}{2}\|\btheta - \btheta^*\|^2$ and a misspecified proxy $r_\bphi(\btheta) = -\tfrac{1}{2}\|\btheta - \bphi\|^2$, where $\bphi$ drifts under adversarial control.
The KL penalty is $\beta\,\tfrac{1}{2}\|\btheta - \btheta_{\mathrm{ref}}\|^2$.
We discretize the nonlinear dynamics using a fourth-order Runge--Kutta scheme with step size $h = 5 \times 10^{-3}$; the LQ game simulations use forward Euler with step size $h = 10^{-2}$.

\paragraph{Phase portraits (Figure~\ref{fig:phase}).}
Figure~\ref{fig:phase} shows phase portraits in the $(z_1, z_2)$ plane for $\beta = 2\beta_c$ (stable spiral, left) and $\beta = 0.5\beta_c$ (limit cycle, right).
The predicted bifurcation threshold is $\beta_c = 0.5000$ (2D) and $\beta_c = 0.6957$ (4D); the observed transitions match exactly, confirming Theorem~\ref{thm:hopf}.

\paragraph{LQ game: gradient play vs.\ Nash feedback.}
We solve the zero-sum ARE (Theorem~\ref{thm:riccati}) via Hamiltonian eigenvalue decomposition and compare the open-loop and closed-loop dynamics.
The \emph{open-loop} (gradient play) Jacobian $\bA(\beta)$ exhibits a Hopf bifurcation at $\beta_c = 0.1304$ with crossing frequency $\omega_c = 0.505$, confirming Theorem~\ref{thm:hopf}.
The \emph{Nash closed-loop} Jacobian $\bA - \bS\bP$ is Hurwitz for all tested $\beta$ (max $\operatorname{Re}(\lambda) \approx -0.45$), confirming that Nash equilibrium feedback always stabilizes.
This validates the key insight of Theorem~\ref{thm:hopf}: reward hacking is a gradient-play phenomenon, and the bifurcation characterizes the gap between gradient methods and equilibrium play.

\paragraph{Role of the LQ and nonlinear models.}
The LQ analysis determines the bifurcation threshold $\beta_c$ and crossing frequency $\omega_c$ from the linearized dynamics---these are \emph{local} properties.
The LQ model diverges for $\beta < \beta_c$; producing stable limit cycles requires nonlinear saturation (cubic terms in the normal form, or softmax boundedness in the bandit game below).

\paragraph{Bifurcation diagram and cycling frequency (Figure~\ref{fig:bifurcation}).}
Figure~\ref{fig:bifurcation}(a) plots the asymptotic oscillation amplitude as a function of $\beta/\beta_c$.
Since the 2D model is itself the Hopf normal form, the $\sqrt{\beta_c - \beta}$ scaling and exponent $0.4983 \approx 0.5$ confirm the integrator's fidelity rather than an independent prediction.
The non-trivial test is the 4D model: the measured cycling frequency at $\beta = 0.8\beta_c$ is $\hat{\omega} = 0.7354$, compared to the linearization-based prediction $\omega_c = 0.7187$ (2.3\% error), validating that Theorem~\ref{thm:hopf} generalizes beyond the normal form.

\paragraph{Schedule comparison (Figure~\ref{fig:bifurcation}b).}
Figure~\ref{fig:bifurcation}(b) compares cumulative regret $\int_0^T \|\bz(t)\|^2\,dt$ for five KL penalty schedules.
Table~\ref{tab:regret} summarizes the results.
The cosine annealing schedule achieves the lowest regret ($1.014$), followed by linear decay ($1.027$) and the PMP schedule ($1.526$).
Constant $\beta = \beta_c$ is worst ($9.830$) due to marginal stability, while $\beta = 1.5\beta_c$ achieves $1.733$.
The PMP schedule underperforms the heuristic decay schedules in this setting; its value lies in explaining \emph{why} annealing helps---the optimal structure near a Hopf bifurcation is a damped oscillation around $\beta_c$ (Remark~1)---rather than in providing a numerically superior schedule.
All non-constant schedules achieve convergence ($\|\bz(T)\| \approx 0$) while maintaining stability throughout.

\paragraph{Softmax alignment game (Figure~\ref{fig:softmax}).}
To test whether alignment cycling persists beyond the polynomial normal form, we construct a game with a softmax policy over $K$ actions with feature vectors $\bm{f}_k$ on the unit circle in $\R^2$.
The policy $\pi_\btheta(k) = \mathrm{softmax}(\btheta^\top \bm{f}_k)$ maximizes $\E_\pi[r_\bphi] - \beta\,\KL(\pi \| \pi_{\mathrm{ref}})$.
The reward model $\bphi \in \R^2$ is corrupted by distributional shift: $\dot{\bphi} = -\delta(\bphi - \bphi^*) + c \bm{J}\,\E_\pi[\bm{f}]$, where $\bm{J}$ is a rotation modeling the assumption that reward model degradation is not aligned with the policy's movement direction.
The rotational coupling $\bm{J}$ plays the same structural role as the skew-symmetric cross-coupling in Theorem~\ref{thm:hopf}; the bifurcation threshold $\beta_c \approx 0.935$ is determined by the linearized dynamics, consistent with the LQ prediction.
The key nonlinear contribution is that softmax saturation (bounded probabilities) produces bounded limit cycles below $\beta_c$ without requiring explicit polynomial saturation terms---the amplitude scaling exponent is $0.58$, close to the $\tfrac{1}{2}$ predicted by the normal-form theory (Figure~\ref{fig:softmax}).

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Game-theoretic RLHF.}
\citet{munos2024nash} formulated RLHF as a two-player game with Nash equilibrium solutions, solved via mirror descent.
\citet{inpo2025} eliminated the inner loop via an implicit reduction.
\citet{starlhf2024} studied minimax self-play formulations.
\citet{casper2023open} surveyed open problems and fundamental limitations of RLHF.
All these works use discrete-time analysis; our continuous-time formulation reveals phenomena (Hopf bifurcations, optimal control schedules) inaccessible to iterate-level reasoning.

\paragraph{Deep learning as dynamical systems.}
\citet{e2017proposal} proposed viewing deep residual networks as discretized ODEs, a perspective formalized by Neural ODEs~\citep{chen2018neural}. \citet{li2018optimal} developed an optimal control approach to deep learning using Pontryagin's maximum principle.
We adapt this ``learning as optimal control'' lens from supervised learning to the RLHF game, replacing a single ODE with a two-player differential game.

\paragraph{Reward hacking and over-optimization.}
\citet{gao2023scaling} empirically characterized reward over-optimization, finding a scaling law between KL budget and excess proxy reward.
Our Theorem~\ref{thm:hopf} offers a complementary mechanistic perspective: in the continuous-time game, the onset of oscillatory over-optimization is a Hopf bifurcation with the universal $O(\sqrt{\beta_c - \beta})$ amplitude scaling.
Whether this mechanism is operative in practical RLHF remains an open question.

\paragraph{Differential games.}
The theory of two-player zero-sum differential games is classical~\citep{basar1999dynamic,isaacs1965differential}.
LQ differential games with coupled Riccati equations have been extensively studied~\citep{engwerda2005lq}.
Our contribution is applying this machinery to the RLHF alignment problem and extracting the bifurcation structure specific to the reward-KL trade-off.

\paragraph{Oscillations in GAN and min-max optimization.}
The cycling instabilities we characterize via Hopf bifurcation have close analogues in GAN training.
\citet{mescheder2017numerics} showed that gradient-based GAN training exhibits rotational dynamics around equilibria, with eigenvalues of the training operator crossing the imaginary axis---the same mechanism underlying our Theorem~\ref{thm:hopf}.
\citet{nagarajan2017gradient} provided local stability analyses of gradient descent in GANs, identifying conditions under which simultaneous gradient play diverges.
\citet{daskalakis2018training} introduced optimistic training methods to dampen these oscillations, and \citet{mertikopoulos2019optimistic} extended optimistic mirror descent to continuous-time saddle-point problems with formal last-iterate convergence guarantees.
Our work differs in providing a precise bifurcation-theoretic characterization---computing the exact critical threshold $\beta_c$, the cycling frequency $\omega_c$, and proving supercriticality via the first Lyapunov coefficient---within a model tailored to the alignment setting.

% 8. CONCLUSION
% ======================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We showed that reward hacking in a continuous-time alignment game is a Hopf bifurcation: gradient play becomes oscillatory when the KL penalty $\beta$ drops below a computable threshold $\beta_c$ (Theorem~\ref{thm:hopf}), while the Nash equilibrium remains stable for all $\beta > 0$ (Theorem~\ref{thm:riccati}).
The bifurcation is supercritical (first Lyapunov coefficient $\ell_1 < 0$), producing bounded limit cycles whose amplitude scales as $O(\sqrt{\beta_c - \beta})$.
This gap between gradient play and equilibrium play provides a precise characterization of the reward-hacking phenomenon observed empirically in RLHF.

Several directions remain open.
First, extending the zero-sum formulation to a \emph{Stackelberg} game would better model the leader-follower structure of policy-then-adversary updates.
Second, incorporating stochastic dynamics---either through It\^o diffusions or stochastic differential games---would capture the noise inherent in minibatch gradient estimation.
Third, scaling the LQ analysis to high-dimensional parameter spaces via random matrix theory or mean-field approximations is necessary for relevance to modern LLMs.
Finally, the Lyapunov coefficient analysis could be extended to higher-dimensional models to determine whether the supercriticality persists, and whether the bifurcation structure survives under more realistic training dynamics.
